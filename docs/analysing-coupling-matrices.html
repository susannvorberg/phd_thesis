PhD thesis: residue-residue contact prediction

2.1 Analysing Coupling Matrices

State-of-the-art contact prediction methods compute a contact score for each residue pair by computing the Frobenius norm of the corresponding coupling matrix \(w_{ij}\). By doing so, valuable information contained in the 20x20 coupling matrix is lost: the qualitative information about which couplings \(\wijab\) contribute most and in whether it is a positive or negative signal.

Detailed analysis of coupling matrices reveals that they are indicative of the underlying evolutionary constraint for a given residue pair.

Figure 2.1: Residue pairs involved in structural constraints.

Considering an ionic interaction between two residues which is evolutionary conserved, will result in pairings of positively charged arginine(R) and lysine(K) residues with either glutamic acid(E) or aspartic acid(D). Pairings of two positively charged residues (e.g. arginine and arginine) or two negative charged residues (e.g glutamic acid with aspartic acid) are very unlikely, as identical charges are repulsive. Given enough evidence in the alignment (sufficient number of sequences and enough variabilty at both positions), coupling matrices reflect these physico-chemical constraints. Figure 2.2 shows the coupling matrix for residue pair i=92 ande j=129 of PDB 1awq chain A. There is a cluster of strong couplings for charged residues arginine(R), glutamic acid(E) and lysine(K). Lysine and glutamic acid are positively coupled wheras lysine and lysine are negatively coupled.

Figure 2.2: Coupling Matrix for residue pair i=92 and j=129 of PDB 1awq chain A. Size of the bubbles represents coupling strength and color represents the direction of coupling: red = positive coupling, blue = negative coupling. Bars at the x-axis represent single potentials for residue i=92 and bars at the y-axis represent single potentials for residue j=129. Height of the bars represents potential strength and color represents positive (red) and negative (blue) values.

Figure 2.3 shows the coupling matrix for a residue pair that is constrained by space. As can be seen in 2.1, phenylalanine takes a lot of space so that only a non bulky amino acid can fit at the other position. This constraint is reflected in the coupling matrix in that there are strong couplings between Alanine(A) and Tryptophane(W) and Glycine(G) and Phenylalanine(F). As there is a negative coupling between Alanine(A) and Phenylalanine(F) it can be assumed that Alanine is already too big to fit in with Phenylalanine(F), whereas it is fine to co-exist with Tryptophane(W).

Figure 2.3: Coupling Matrix for residue pair i=27 and j=77 of PDB 1atg chain A. Size of the bubbles represents coupling strength and color represents the direction of coupling: red = positive coupling, blue = negative coupling. Bars at the x-axis represent single potentials for residue i=27 and bars at the y-axis represent single potentials for residue j=77. Height of the bars represents potential strength and color represents positive (red) and negative (blue) values.

Another example is shown in Figure 2.4. Here the coupling matrix reflects aromatic interactions between two residues. Tryptophan(W) is strongly positively coupled with Tryptophan(W), as well as Phenylalanine(F) with Tyrosin(Y).

Size of the bubbles represents coupling strength and color represents the direction of coupling: red = positive coupling, blue = negative coupling. Bars at the x-axis represent single potentials for residue i=110 and bars at the y-axis represent single potentials for residue j=160. Height of the bars represents potential strength and color represents positive (red) and negative (blue) values.

There are many more interaction constraints (e.g. pi-cation, aroamtic-proline interactions) as well as volume constraints (e.g large vs small residues) that can be inferred from the patterns revealed by coupling matrices.
