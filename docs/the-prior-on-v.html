3.5 The prior on \(\v\)

Most previous approaches chose a prior around the origin, \(p(\v) = \N ( \v| \mathbf{0}, \lambda_v \I)\). This choice has an obvious draw-back. To see why, we take the sum over \(b=1,\ldots, 20\) of eq. (3.3) at the optimum, where the gradient vanishes.

This yields \[\begin{equation} 0 = \sum_{n=1}^N I(x_{ni} \eq a, x_{nj} \ne 0) - N_{ij}\, p(x_i \eq a | \v, \w) - \lambda_w \sum_{b=1}^{20} \wijab. \end{equation}\] Incidentally, we note that by taking the sum over \(a\) we find \[\begin{equation} \sum_{a,b=1}^{20} \wijab = 0. \label{eq:sum_wijab=0} \end{equation}\] At the optimum the gradient with respect to \(v_{ia}\) vanishes and we can substitute \(p(x_i=a|\v,\w) = q(x_i=a) - \lambda_v (\via - \via^*) / N_i\), yielding
\[\begin{eqnarray} 0 = N_{ij} \left( q(x_i=a, x_j \ne 0) - q(x_i=a) \right) + \frac{N_{ij}}{N_i} \lambda_v(\via - \via^*) - \lambda_w \sum_{b=1}^{20} \wijab . \label{eq:gauge_opt} \end{eqnarray}\]

for all \(i,j \in \{1,\ldots,L\}\) and all \(a \in \{1,\ldots,20\}\). To show that the choice \(\v^*= \bs 0\) leads to undesirable results, we take an MSA without gaps (therefore the first term on the right hand side vanishes) and consider a column \(i\) that is not coupled to any other. Assume that amino acid \(a\) was frequent in column \(i\) and therefore \(\via\) would be large and positive. Then according to eq.~, for any other column \(j\) the 20 coefficients \(\wijab\) for \(b \in \{1,\ldots,20\}\) would have to take up the bill and deviate from zero!

To correct this unwanted behaviour, we instead chose a Gaussian prior centered around \(\v^*\) obeying \[\begin{equation} \frac{\exp(\via^*)}{\sum_{a'=1}^{20} \exp(v_{ia'}^*)} = q(x_i=a) . \end{equation}\] This choice ensures that if no columns are coupled, i.e. \(p(\x|\v,\w) = \prod_{i=1}^L p(x_i)\), \(\v=\v^*\) and \(\w= \bs 0\) gives the correct probability model for the sequences in the MSA. If we impose the restraint \(\sum_{a=1}^{20} \via = 0\) to fix the gauge of the \(\via\) (i.e. to remove the indeterminacy), we get

For this choice, \(\via - \via^*\) will be approximately zero and will certainly be much smaller than \(\via\), hence the sum over coupling coefficients in eq. will be close to zero, as it should be. Another way to understand the choice of \(\v^*\) in eq. as opposed to \(\v^*=\bs 0\) is by noting that in that case \(q(x_i=a) \approx p(x_i=a|\v^*,\w^*)\). Therefore, if \(q(x_i=a,x_j=b) = q(x_i=a) \, q(x_j=b)\) it follows that \(p(x_{i}=a,x_{j}=b|\v,\w) \approx q(x_i=a,x_j=b) = p(x_i=a|\v^*,\w^*)\, p(x_j=b|\v^*,\w^*)\), i.e. we would correctly conclude that \(\wijab=0\) and \((i,a)\) and \((j,b)\) are not coupled.
