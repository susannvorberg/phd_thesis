2.3 Signals in Coupling Matrices with respect to Distance

The qualitative trends of coupling values observed in single coupling matrices can be quantified by looking at how the distribution of single coupling values from many residue pairs. To avoid uninformative couplings, we consider only residue pairs with a sequence separation > 10 and with enough evidence for a certain amino acid pairing (see Methods ??).

Figure 2.6 shows the distribution of selected single coupling terms for residue pairs within a \(\Cb\) distance of 0\(\AA\) - 8\(\AA\). The distribution of \(w_{ij}(E,E)\) terms is shifted and skewed towards negative values, in accordance with repulsive interactions between two negatively charged gluatamic acid residues. In contrast, the distribution for \(w_{ij}(R,E)\) terms is shifted and skewed towards positive values, reflecting the attracting electrostatic interactions between positively charged arginine and negatively charged gluatamic acid residues. Disulfide bonds have a broad distribution that is skewed towards positive values, reflecting the strong signals obtained from usually strictly conserved cysteins. Coupling terms for hydrophobic pairs like I-L have a symmetric coupling distribution, therefoe being equally favoured and disfavoured by hydrophobic partners. Hydrophobic interactions are not very specific and can easily be substituted by other hydrophobic residues, which explains the not very pronounced positive coupling signal compared to more specific interactions, e.g ionic interactions.

Coupling terms \(w_{ij}(W,F)\), exemplary for aromatic interactions also have a symmetric distribution. Aromatic residues are very bulky and aromatic interactions can occur over long ranges. Therefore at smaller distances more often negative coupling values due to steric hindrance.
