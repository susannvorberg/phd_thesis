PhD thesis: residue-residue contact prediction

2.1 Physico-Chemical Interaction Fingerprints in Coupling Matrices

Figure 2.1 visualizes the inferred coupling matrix for a residue pair (residues 6 and 82) in protein 1awq, chain A. Clearly visible is a cluster of strong couplings for charged and polar residues (E,D,K,R,Q). Positive couplings (in red) can be observed between positively charged residues (R,K) and negatively charged residues (E,D), whereas couplings between equally charged residues are negative. The coupling matrix perfectly reflects the interaction preference for residues forming salt bridges. Indeed, in the protein structure residue 6 (glutamic acid) forms a salt bridge with residue 82 (lysine) as can be seen in Figure 2.3.

Figure 2.1: Coupling Matrix for residue pair i=6 and j=82 of PDB 1awq chain A. Size of the bubbles represents coupling strength and color represents the direction of coupling: red = positive coupling, blue = negative coupling. Bars at the x-axis represent single potentials for residue i=6 and bars at the y-axis represent single potentials for residue j=82. Height of the bars represents potential strength and color represents positive (red) and negative (blue) values.

Figure 2.2 visualizes the coupling matrix for a pair of hydrophobic residues (residues 29 and 39) in protein 1ae9 chain A. The coupling matrix reveals strong couplings for hydrophobic amino acids but also reflects a sterical constraint: alanine as a small hydrophobic residue is favoured at either position 29 or position 39, but disfavoured to appear at both positions. Positions 29 and 39 are in the core of the protein where hydrophobic residues are densely packed. Figure 2.3 shows the two residues in the protein core and the limited space that allows for only small hydrophobic residues.

Figure 2.2: Coupling Matrix for residue pair i=29 and j=39 of PDB 1ae9 chain A. Size of the bubbles represents coupling strength and color represents the direction of coupling: red = positive coupling, blue = negative coupling. Bars at the x-axis represent single potentials for residue i=29 and bars at the y-axis represent single potentials for residue j=39. Height of the bars represents potential strength and color represents positive (red) and negative (blue) values.

Figure 2.3: Interactions between protein side chains. Left: residue 6 (glutamic acid) forming a salt bridge with residue 82 (lysine) in protein 1awq, chain A. Right: residue 29 (alanine) and residue 39 (leucine) within the hydrophobic core of protein 1ae9 chain A.

We could identify many more biological interpretable signals in coupling matrices, including pi-cation interactions (see Appendix C.1), aromatic-proline interactions (see Appendix C.3), sulfur-aromtic interactions or disulfide bonds (see Appendix C.2).

Coucke and collegues [47] performed a thorough quantitative analysis of coupling matrices selected from confidently predicted contacts. They performed a spectral analysis of averaged coupling matrices revealing eigenmodes which are closely related to amino acid interactions with specific physico-chemical properties, like electrostaticity, hydrophobicity or steric interactions or disulfide bonds. Their study confirms the obvious intuition that physico-chemical interactions leave characteristic fingerprints in coupling matrices.
